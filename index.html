Intro & motivation

2 questions
Q01 Tell us about yourself and why you're applying — specifically to the Design Lab.

I studied design in college, and right after I graduated I worked as a UX designer for about two years. So I already had a pretty solid digital design background when I got to ITP. But being here pushed me to also get good at physical design — thinking about form, materials, how something feels in your hand. Now those two things together are kind of at the center of how I work.

The reason I really want to be at the Design Lab is that I've noticed a gap that I think I'm in a good spot to fill. The Lab does great work on the digital side, but there's a real need around physical design that isn't being met. I know this because I started posting my work on Instagram, and I got DMs from over ten ITP students — mostly first-years — all basically saying the same thing: "I want to talk about physical design." They were working on physical computing projects and had no idea how to think about the casing, the form, the material. I started doing informal one-on-ones with them, and it made it really clear — there's a need here that no one is filling. I want to be that person.

Start strong, then get to the community story quickly. That's what makes you stand out.

The physical design gap

3 questions
Q03 What do you see as the biggest gap in design support at ITP right now?

Physical design. When students working on physical computing projects need help, they usually go to Phil or Ian in the shop — and that's great for the technical and materials side of things. But there's a step before that that nobody's really covering: the design thinking. Like, how do you figure out the form of an enclosure so it actually says the right thing? What material fits the tone of your project? How do you think about color and texture as part of the experience?

That design layer is just kind of missing right now. And I saw this really clearly when I was doing those informal sessions with students. They weren't asking "how do I use the laser cutter" — they were asking "how do I make this thing feel right." That's a design question. Phil and Ian are amazing but that's not really their territory — and that's exactly where I can step in.

Make it clear you're adding to what Phil and Ian do, not criticizing them. It's about filling a gap, not replacing anyone.

Q04 You mentioned running informal office hours around physical design. Tell us more about that.

It kind of happened on its own. I started posting my work on Instagram — stuff that mixed digital and physical design — and I started getting DMs from ITP students, mostly first-years. They'd seen my work and wanted to talk through their own projects. And the thing that kept coming up over and over was physical design: how to think about the form, the casing, the materials for their physical computing work.

I ended up doing one-on-ones with more than ten people. I'd look at what they were building, ask questions about what the object needed to say, and help them think through the form, what material made sense, and how the physical design could actually support the interaction they were going for.

What really hit me was how much it helped, and how obvious it was that this was something no one else was offering. That's when I realized — this isn't just something I could do. It's something people here are actively looking for.

Q05 How do you actually help a student think through physical design? Walk us through your process.

I always start with intent — before anything else. What should this object feel like when you pick it up? What does it need to communicate before you even touch it? A project about something intimate and a project about something precise should feel really different from each other, and that difference should be a choice, not something that just happened.

From there I work through the form — the shape, the proportions, whether it should feel soft or sharp. Then the material: not just "what can I make this out of" but "what does this material actually say." Matte versus glossy, warm versus cold, stiff versus flexible — all of that carries meaning. Then color comes in.

The nice thing about having worked in the shop is that I can take all of that design thinking and connect it directly to what's actually possible to build. I know what machines work for what shapes, what materials behave like what, and I can sit with a student and help them go all the way from "here's what I want it to feel like" to actually making it. That's the full loop, and I can close it.

Design thinking

2 questions
Q06 How do you think about design in the context of ITP projects? What does design thinking mean to you here?

At ITP, design has to work across both digital and physical — and honestly, that's what makes it so interesting here. Most design programs only teach one or the other. But ITP students are building things that live in both worlds at once: a project might have a screen, a physical enclosure, and some kind of tactile feedback, and all three need to feel like they belong together.

For me, design thinking at ITP starts with one question: what should this make someone feel? Before they even touch it — what does it communicate? That question works the same whether you're designing a UI or a physical form. A project about closeness should feel different from a project about control — in the materials, the shapes, the surface, the color — and those should all be real decisions, not just accidents.

What I see a lot is students with really great concepts who then kind of underdesign the physical side. The idea is there, but the object doesn't quite carry it yet. That gap is where I want to work — helping people translate what they mean into something you can actually see and touch.

Teaching & workshops

2 questions
Q10 How would you support first-year students and undergraduates?

The thing I see most with first-years is that they have a really clear idea of what they want to make, but they get stuck on how to actually make it physical. I try to meet them right there — asking things like: what should this feel like when you hold it? What material gets that across? How can the way it works reinforce what it's trying to say?

For undergrads, the main thing is usually confidence. I focus on getting something built fast — even rough — so they can hold it, see it, get some feedback, and go back. That back-and-forth is what actually builds confidence, way more than planning everything out first. I try not to push my own answers on anyone — I want them to find their own way there.

Saved ✓
Q11 What workshops would you want to run as a resident?

Two main tracks. The first is physical design workshops — sessions where we actually work through form, materials, color, texture as design decisions. These would be for students who have a solid concept but don't know how to give it a physical shape. I'd structure them so that you walk in with an idea and walk out with something you can hold. That kind of quick, tangible result is really useful.

The second track is design tools — mainly Figma, but also Illustrator. I've already done this twice at ITP in small groups. The key is teaching it so it connects to how you actually think about design, not just how to use the software.

What I'd really love to build eventually is a workshop that bridges both — you start with a concept, design it digitally in Figma, and then make a physical version by the end. Something like: idea → Figma → fabricated form, all in one session. That kind of workshop doesn't really exist here yet and I think it would be really valuable.

C01 Talk about your design process — how do you think about interactions with your pieces?

My process always starts before I touch any tool. The first thing I ask is — what kind of feeling should this create? What should someone feel the second they see it, before they even do anything? That question is what drives everything else.

For physical pieces especially, I think about interaction like a conversation between the object and the person. What does the shape invite? What does the material suggest? When you reach for something with soft edges and a matte surface, you already have an idea of how it's going to feel — and I want that expectation to be intentional. I try to design the moment before the interaction, not just the interaction itself.

A concrete example from my haptic work: I don't start by thinking about vibration patterns. I start with the emotion — something like "a quiet kind of reassurance" — and work backward from there into the physical details: the rhythm, how strong it is, how long it lasts, the pauses in between. The whole process is about turning a feeling into a set of real decisions. And that's true whether I'm working on a UI, a physical object, or a haptic system.

C02 What's the importance of design thinking in ITP projects, and how would you bring that to the community as a resident?

Design thinking is basically the thing that holds all of that together. What I see most often is students who have a genuinely strong concept but then kind of underdesign the execution. The idea is solid, but the object or the interface doesn't quite carry it yet. Design thinking is what gives you the tools to close that gap — to turn what you mean into something you can actually see and touch and use.

As a resident, I'd bring this in two ways. First, just being there — being the person students come to when they're stuck between having an idea and figuring out how to give it form. Asking the design questions that help them find their own answers. Second, through workshops that make design thinking something you can actually learn and practice — physical design sessions where we work through form, material, and color as real decisions, not just visual choices. I want it to feel like a natural part of how everyone works here, not some extra thing.
